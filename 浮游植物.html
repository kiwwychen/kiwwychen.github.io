Exploring Phytoplankton in the Ocean

Why phytoplankton is important?

    Every year, phytoplankton consume approximately 50% of the global plant's carbon dioxide, representing an equivalent carbon
absorption to land plants. When phytoplankton die, some of the carbon is transportedto the deep sea, and when they areconsumed
by other organisms, some carbon is transferred to different layers of
the ocean.
    This 'biological carbon pump' can annually transfer around 1 billion metric tons of carbon from the atmosphere to the
deep sea worldwide. This process completes carbon cycling, so even minor fluctuations in phytoplankton growth can potentially impact the concentration of carbon dioxide in the atmosphere, thereby providing feedback to global surface temperatures.

Will climate change affect algae size?

    NASA's research has explored the impact of climate change on the distribution of diatoms, which are a key functional group of phytoplankton and representative of large phytoplankton.
    The study findings indicate that climate change is leading to increased nutrient scarcity in the surface oceans, which favors the growth of small phytoplankton while posing challenges for the survival of diatoms. Therefore, global climate change is unfavorable for large algae.

Variety of Phytoplankton:

Cyanobacteria-The god of origan
    Cyanobacteria are the most primitive organisms on Earth. Some species of cyanobacteria have cells that aggregate and connect to form filamentous colonies. They contain pigments like phycocyanin and chlorophyll, giving them various colors. Due to the protective outer mucilaginous sheath, they can thrive in diverse environments.

Cyanobacteria

Diatoms-The god of food
    Diatoms known as silica algae because of the significant weight of silica in their shells, are one of the primary food sources for aquatic organisms. They can be classified into two categories based on the arrangement of their patterns: centric diatoms with radial symmetry and pennate diatoms with bilateral symmetry.

Flagellates-The god of death
    Flagellates can produce toxins, and if they proliferate excessively, they can lead to harmful algal blooms (HABs), which can paralyze or even be fatal to humans when consuming contaminated seafood.Flagellates have two flagella, one flagellum provides them with the ability for rotational movement, while the other controls their direction of motion. Some flagellates closely resemble animals and obtain nutrients by ingesting plants.

Dinoflagellata

Coccolithophores-The god of war
    The outer layer of coccolithophores is covered with various calcium carbonate plates just like armor. When they die, these outer layers form calcium carbonate sediment, completing the carbon cycle. In recent years, scientists have discovered that coccolithophores can release condensation nuclei, allowing the atmosphere to absorb moisture and form clouds, which can reduce the amount of sunlight entering the ocean.
